## Round 1

**Sort these words from the wardrobe**

`BREECHES` ┬╖ `PANTALOONS` ┬╖ `PANTS` ┬╖ `FANCY PANTS`

### Reveal (oldest тЖТ newest)

1. **BREECHES** тАФ Old English (~800s) тАФ Among the oldest clothing words in English. Trousers reaching to or just below the knee.
2. **PANTALOONS** тАФ 1660s тАФ Named after Pantalone, a greedy, foolish old man in Italian commedia dell'arte who wore tight trousers under a jacket.
3. **PANTS** тАФ 1835 тАФ Shortened from pantaloons. Ambrose Bierce called it "vulgar exceedingly." In British slang by the 1990s, it means rubbish.
4. **FANCY PANTS** тАФ 1870 тАФ First used to describe a type of fabric. Later became a playful insult, then an all-purpose adjective. Smarty pants followed in the 1930s.

## Round 2

**Sort these words that went from quirky to grumpy**

`CROTCHET` ┬╖ `CRANK` ┬╖ `CRANKY` ┬╖ `CROTCHETY`

### Reveal (oldest тЖТ newest)

1. **CROTCHET** тАФ 1400s тАФ Borrowed from French for a small ornamental hook. By the 1500s, it also meant a whimsical fancy or odd notion, as in "whims and crotchets."
2. **CRANK** тАФ late 1500s тАФ Started as the bent part of an axle. Then became a fanciful turn of speech, as in "quips and cranks." By the mid-1800s, a person with eccentric hobbies.
3. **CRANKY** тАФ early 1800s тАФ Shifted from "prone to eccentric notions" to simply "difficult to please" and "ill-tempered."
4. **CROTCHETY** тАФ mid 1800s тАФ Originally meant "full of crotchets" тАФ given to whims or eccentricities. Underwent pejoration and now just means grouchy.

## Round 3

**Sort these words for "right exactly there"**

`SLAP BANG` ┬╖ `LICKETY SPLIT` ┬╖ `SMACK DAB` ┬╖ `BANG ON`

### Reveal (oldest тЖТ newest)

1. **SLAP BANG** тАФ late 1700s тАФ British expression meaning vigorously, without delay, or directly in terms of position. "Slap bang in the center."
2. **LICKETY SPLIT** тАФ 1818 тАФ American term for at full speed. The "lick" comes from the verb meaning to move fast. An early critic called it a "clumsy expression."
3. **SMACK DAB** тАФ 1839 тАФ Exactly in position. An American cousin of "slap bang." Has been rising in usage since the 1980s.
4. **BANG ON** тАФ 1930s тАФ British slang for exactly right or excellent. Related to the same family of impact words: slap, smack, dab, bang.

## Round 4

**Sort these words from the pepper plant**

`PEPPER (verb)` ┬╖ `PEPPERY` ┬╖ `PEP` ┬╖ `PEPPY`

### Reveal (oldest тЖТ newest)

1. **PEPPER (verb)** тАФ 1500s тАФ To cover a surface with small objects or to pelt with small missiles. A face "peppered with freckles." A piece "peppered with typos."
2. **PEPPERY** тАФ 1700s тАФ Having a hot temper, or content that's provocative. One 1778 letter called a man a "peppery, hotheaded, inflammable, combustible powder mill fellow."
3. **PEP** тАФ 1908 тАФ Shortened from pepper. First appeared in the Washington Post about baseball: "miney little pep in the Giants' game." Gave us pep talk, pep rally, pep squad.
4. **PEPPY** тАФ 1914 тАФ Full of energy and high spirits. First used to describe a coach. Still going strong.

## Round 5

**Sort these words born from luck**

`HAP` ┬╖ `MISHAP` ┬╖ `PERHAPS` ┬╖ `HAPPY`

### Reveal (oldest тЖТ newest)

1. **HAP** тАФ c. 1200 тАФ Meant chance or fortune. Borrowed into English from Old Norse. Could mean either good or bad luck depending on context.
2. **MISHAP** тАФ 1300s тАФ Literally "bad fortune." The prefix mis- plus hap. Still used today, though most people don't connect it to luck.
3. **PERHAPS** тАФ 1400s тАФ Literally "by chance." The per means "by" plus hap. We still use it every day without knowing we're talking about fortune.
4. **HAPPY** тАФ 1500s тАФ Originally meant "experiencing good fortune," not contentment. The shift from lucky to joyful happened gradually. And that's where we get happy holidays тАФ wishes for good fortune.
